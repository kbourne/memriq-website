04 — Definition

Agentic engineering, defined.

Specifications, not snippets.You write specs dense enough that agents can execute them.
Systems, not lines of code.You design how the parts fit, not how each line reads.
Judgment is the human's job.You stay in the loop for system design, verification, and taste.
The PRD is the primary artifact.Product requirements as executable input — not a doc someone reads after.

07 — Human edges

Where human judgment still wins.

  • Ambiguous problems agents will confidently get wrong.
  • System design across boundaries agents can't see.
  • Knowing when to stop — when a solution is wrong enough to scrap.
  • Designing evaluations — what does “good” even mean here?
  • Taste. The thing you can't write down.

09 — For MADS

MADS didn't teach you to compete with agents.
It taught you the skills agents depend on.

  • Understanding what's under the model — not just calling APIs.
  • NLP foundations — retrieval, embeddings, tokenization, why they behave that way.
  • Eval design — measure it or it didn't happen.
  • Statistical intuition — knowing when a result is real.
  • Problem decomposition — the precondition for writing PRDs agents can execute.
The technical foundations matter — but the deeper value is that MADS taught you to think and work like a data scientist. It's why my code ships cleaner. Why my decisions are data-driven. Why I frame problems differently than engineering-only colleagues. The way of thinking is what compounds — and it's the thing agents can't replicate.

10 — The experiment

I don't know where this is going.
I'm running an experiment to find out.

  • Memriq.ai — my thought experiment. What if you pushed to the limit and built a company with only agents? Not a prediction. A way to learn what's possible.
  • My actual belief: push humans up the value equation. Less typing, less repetition. More judgment, taste, design, framing.
  • The point isn't replacement. It's about humans operating at the top of their stack.
